LinkedIn / ux motion initiative

Leading and defining LinkedIn's UX motion initiative from the ground up — a twelve-week effort to audit the flagship mobile app, design key interaction patterns, and templatize a scalable motion library for their design systems.

UX Motion Design Systems Flagship Mobile App 12 weeks
linkedin/hero.mp4 — motion at work in a real product surface
LinkedIn · 01 / 08
LinkedIn · 02 / 08 — overview
TeamUED Design Systems — Sunnyvale
ToolsAfter Effects, Sketch, Webflow
DurationTwelve weeks
RoleVisual & Motion Designer
ProblemThe flagship mobile app made static, abrupt interface changes — foregoing UX motion opportunities throughout the experience.
ObjectiveBuild a UX motion library as a guide for designers and engineers to implement motion into the flagship mobile app.
SolutionAudit the app for motion opportunities, design compositions that showcase UX value, and templatize them to streamline implementation.
ResultPresented to leadership and well received — engineering and design resources are now allocated to UX motion as a design-systems pillar.

LinkedIn · 04 / 08 — proposal

Motion is often dismissed as a gloss layer. In truth, it can remove uncertainty, improve discoverability, hide latency, and build spatial and navigational relationships.

01

Gesture Confirmation

Acknowledge input so an action feels registered and intentional.

02

Hiding Latency

Mask wait time with motion so the system never feels stalled.

03

Motion Affordance

Hint at what is interactive and what an action will do next.

04

Spatial & Navigational

Establish where things come from and where they go.

Intuit · 06 / 06 — takeaway

Two motion systems, one coherent product.

UX motion did the usability work — hiding latency, confirming actions, and guiding attention — while traditional animation principles carried Intuit's brand character. The craft was keeping the two from compromising each other.

Key takeaway

Functional motion and brand motion can share one component library — if each is designed to respect the other.

BMW · 02 / 04 — the problem

The starting point: type every search — while driving.

BMW's original in-car search lived on the center-console touchscreen, with an on-screen keyboard.

  • Drivers search each destination, letter by letter.
  • Eyes on the screen and a hand off the wheel.
  • Basic, slow, and distracting in motion.
  • Far from the premium, driver-friendly BMW standard.

The original input — typing a destination on the center-console touchscreen.

BMW · 04 / 04 — result & impact
  • Drivers understood the search status at every moment — visual, auditory, and motion cues working together.
  • Invisible system states made visible — ready, listening, or working, at a glance.
  • Less repetition and hesitation, because status was never ambiguous.
  • A calmer, more trustworthy experience, with minimal distraction.

Smart thermostat app. The original screen looks minimal — but the interface relies on the user to infer too much.

Before Redesign
redesign/laws-of-ux-audit.png — Laws of UX audit

Visually simple, mentally ambiguous — the screen offloads too much inference onto the user.

Thermostat · key ux problems

Three things break the moment.

1

Current vs. target temperature is unclear.

2

System state is under-explained.

3

Mode, fan, and schedule clutter the main control moment.

Thermostat · reference & direction

Calm minimalism, with clarity added.

The redesign keeps the calm minimalism of the reference, but improves clarity through labels, hierarchy, and progressive disclosure.

Thermostat · redesign strategy

From inference to clarity.

Before
  • — Current temp dominates the screen
  • — Target temperature is unclear
  • — Secondary controls always visible
After
  • + Current + target clarified
  • + System state explained in plain language
  • + Large dial control, front and center
  • + Secondary controls hidden behind one button

Thermostat · motion design

Every beat confirms the action.

Each motion and haptic beat is a deliberate confirmation of the user's action — turning a simple temperature change into a sequence of clear, reassuring feedback.

  1. 01
    Slider scales up on touch
    Gesture confirmation & motion affordance.
  2. 02
    Haptic tick on engage
    A physical cue the control is now live.
  3. 03
    Digits animate as they change
    Makes each numeric step obvious.
  4. 04
    Haptic on release
    Confirms the new temperature is set.
  5. 05
    Header shifts to “Heating”
    System state changes in plain language.
  6. 06
    Notification confirms activation
    Reassures the system is running.

Thermostat · design principle
Key takeaway

Minimalism works best when it reduces ambiguity, not information — and considered motion and haptics turn every interaction into a moment of confidence, confirming each action as it happens.
